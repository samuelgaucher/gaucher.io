Ongoing research

I currently work on the growth by molecular-beam epitaxy of semiconductor heterostructures with ferromagnetic materials. The project adds to distributed efforts in the field of spintronics, the goal being to implement devices in which the electronic spin degree of freedom can be harnessed to achieve denser information processing. You can track our progress through the publications posted below. In 2017, I created a new 2D material which I am now also investigating.

MBE

Molecular-beam epitaxy (MBE) is a technique to create single-crystalline thin-films by deposition of atoms under ultra-high vacuum. The strength of MBE lies in the capacity it offers to engineer crystals with sub-nanometer precision, hence creating nanostructures in which quantum mechanical effects are revealed and made (more easily) accessible. MBE has been instrumental in expanding our knowledge of semiconductors, and paved new ways for research in physics, materials science, and nanoelectronics.

Interests

My scientific interests span a range of subjects: growth by MBE and physics of low-dimensional systems, spin phenomena in semiconductor nanostructures, electrical transport in magnetic semiconductors, integer and fractional quantum Hall effects. I wish to look deeper into topological quantum condensed matter, quantum anomalous and spin Hall effects, chiral Majorana fermions, and the ways in which all the above can be applied to quantum information technology.



Publications

Ferromagnet/semiconductor/ferromagnet hybrid trilayers grown using solid-phase epitaxy

S. Gaucher et al., Semicond. Sci. Technol. 33 104005 (2018)

We're now able to grow lattice-matched semiconductors over both Fe3Si and Co2FeSi. Since the two ferromagnetic materials have different coercive fields, it is possible to control the orientation of their magnetization independently. This means a lot to produce devices that could be operated as spin-selective transistors.

Ordered structure of FeGe2 formed during solid-phase epitaxy

B. Jenichen, M. Hanke, S. Gaucher, et al. Phys. Rev. Mater. 2 051402 (2018)

Under certain conditions, a new 2D polymorph of FeGe2 can form by slowly heating a thin amorphous Ge layer over Fe3Si, which features 2D planes of monolayer Fe atoms separated by tetragonal Ge bilayers. The ordering phenomenon is interpreted as a minimization of the elastic energy of the epitaxial film.

Specific heat and entropy of fractional quantum Hall states in the second Landau level

B. A. Schmidt, K. Bennaceur, S. Gaucher, et al., Phys. Rev. B 95 201306 (2017)

The 5/2 state of the fractional quantum Hall effect is especially difficult to probe, but could host quasiparticles obeying non-Abelian fractional statistics. An investigation of the specific heat in this state could be a way to measure its entropy and shed light on the nature of its statistical properties.

Growth of Fe3Si/Ge/Fe3Si trilayers on GaAs(001) using solid-phase epitaxy

S. Gaucher et al., Appl. Phys. Lett. 110 102103 (2017)

We realized one of the rare examples of a semiconductor grown lattice-matched over a metallic substrate. The different crystallization energies problem was bypassed by developing a solid-phase epitaxy approach, whereby a thin amorphous Ge film is slowly annealed over ferromagnetic Fe3Si. Being able to obtain such magnetic tunnel junctions, where the barrier consists of a semiconductor, is a first step towards the realization of transistor-like devices that are also spin-sensitive.

Mechanical Flip-Chip for Ultra-High Electron Mobility Devices

K. Bennaceur, B. A. Schmidt, S. Gaucher, et al., Sci. Rep. 5 13494 (2015)

Extremely-high mobility two-dimensional electron gases are required to probe the exotic physics of the fractional quantum Hall effect. Since most nanofabrication techniques are invasive and irreversible, a flip-chip approach was developed to leave the samples in pristine conditions.
